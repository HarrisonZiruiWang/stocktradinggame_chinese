Realized and Unrealized Gain/Loss Description

When trading, there is an important distinction between realized and unrealized gains and losses. In general, when traders buy a stock and its value changes, they have an unrealized gain or loss as long as they have not yet sold that stock. Once they sell the stock, they will have a realized gain or loss.

For example, if Lisa buys 2 shares of Stock A for $50 each and Stock A goes up in value to $56, we say Lisa has an unrealized gain of $12 ($6 per share x 2 shares). If Lisa immediately sold her 2 shares of Stock A for $56 each, she would realize her gain of $12. At that point we would say Lisa has a realized gain of $12.

If John bought 3 shares of Stock B for $80 each and Stock B went down in value to $71, we say John has an unrealized loss of $27 ($9 per share x 3 shares). If John immediately sold 2 shares of Stock B for $71 each, he would have a realized loss of $18 ($9 per share x 2 shares). He would also have an unrealized loss of $9 because he still has not sold 1 share of Stock B which is at a loss of $9.

Stock Price Development

Stock prices change each round according to the following two processes. First, it will be determined whether a stock will exhibit a price increase or decrease. The probabilities of increases or decreases are different for each stock and do not change between rounds.

Second, the magnitude of the price increase or decrease will be determined. Prices either change by $1, $3, or $5 with the same probability. The magnitude of the price changes is completely independent of whether the price is increasing or decreasing.

The performance of a stock during 1 iteration of the game has nothing to do with its performance in other iterations of the game. Also, your choices do not affect the price development.
